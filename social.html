About the Project

People Power

In the early days, media coverage and the spread of information was restricted to media outlooks such as newspapers and new television shows that only broadcasted news that they saw fit. The internet has vastly changed that with the emergence of YouTube which was a monumental shift from traditional media to broadcasting to user-based submissions. This gave the average individual the ability to broadcast their thoughts and opinions to a greater audience without having a big media deem it worthy.

With the cultural shift to user-based media with YouTube, came a shift in written based media as well with the site digg.com. Digg is a news aggregator with a curated front page, aiming to select stories specifically for the Internet audience. It chooses its stories based on the user's votes and does not rely on an editor or producer to deem a story interesting.

In addition to personal broadcasting sites came the innovation of MP3 compression sparked computer-based music players. The ability to compress music files to a feasible size was revolutionary for using computers to listen to music. This innovation was highly ignored by the music companies as they did not see it as a threat and decided it would never become a big deal as opposed to traditional means. This left a void on the internet for people who wanted to listen to digital music but had no outlet to obtain it to start file sharing online.

A college student named Shawn Fanning saw the need and created Napster which was peer to peer file sharing. Napster did not end intellectual property but it had set music free from the distribution monopoly that the huge record companies had controlled for years. Big names like Dr. Dre and Metallica along with music industry lawyers fought against it. Courts found Napster illegal and made it shutdown.

Napster may have gained its popularity through illegal copyright means which inevitably caused its downfall, they had inspired the revolution of the creation of copyright protected stores like iTunes which sold individual songs for ninety-nine cents each. As a result, more inspired peer to peer software such as LimeWire and Kazaa were invented to fill the void of Napster.

With the rise of YouTube and Digg, content created by the crowd had become known as Web 2.0. This ranged from Friendster, to MySpace, Twitter and Facebook which were interactive / social media centric internet sites. The content on these sites were made by the users of the site. The popularity of these sites was enormous and instrumental to Web 2.0. Facebook being at the pinnacle of the success of these social media sites began aggressively expanding like opening up an internal development platform for ads and mini applications.

Along with the evaluation of Facebook, came the emergence of Craigslist and Wikipedia. Wikipedia was created with hopes of user curated and maintained encyclopedia entries online and Craigslist eliminating need for classified and Peer to peer ads that newspaper outlets had profited on for many years.

The Dot Com bubble was very monumental for individuals, it not only disrupted almost every industry but it removed some of the power news outlets or music industry companies held securely for years. As a result, the music industry had to change dramatically from their old ways of selling music while the news industry lost revenue and their complete monopoly on the distribution of information.
