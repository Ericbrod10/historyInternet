Browser Wars

The internet that we know today was nothing like how it was in its early stages. When you think of the internet, the word “browser” along with a few others comes to mind. This is not a coincidence since the creation of the internet browser played a key role in the evolution of the modern-day internet and is regularly used to access it today. The creation of the internet browser was not looked at favorably by all however, Microsoft a large and growing software company saw a browser-based internet as a threat to Windows OS which had been on 90% of the computer in the world at the time.

Microsoft’s co-founder and CEO, Bill Gates had a different and more monetary inclined vision of the evolution of the internet. His vision thought the information highway that was the internet, should be a toll road where Microsoft controlled the toll booths and the users would have to pay to drive/access information. This would allow Microsoft to have a complete monopoly on information and give them the ability to censor and restrict content that they did not approve of. Gate’s online system was called the Microsoft network (msn) which many assumed would succeed. Microsoft’s motivation for this idea was out of fear that some small software companies could threaten everything Microsoft has achieved like it had done to so many before.

The first graphical web browser was Mosaic founded by Illinois University students. Jim Clark, the co-founder of Netscape, had recruited the Mosaic engineers. They would go on to create a new browser based on Mosaic and turn the internet into the future for commerce and communication.

The moment Netscape’s browser “Navigator” was released, it was an instant technological success. The internet age had begun and it did not have anything to do with Microsoft. Microsoft’s fears of the threat had begun to become a reality, Netscape had opened the door to the possibilities where the browser could be a separate alternative to Windows and all computational needs could be handled online rendering Windows OS irreverent. Netscape’s success had become something impossible for Microsoft to overlook anymore and now had to react to this apparent threat and destroy it.

After meeting with Netscape and being accused of breaching Antitrust Laws, Microsoft had started developing their own browser called Internet Explorer (IE) where they would copy Netscape’s foundational ideas and updates. Their plan was to slowly but surely chip away at Netscape’s browser market share. Microsoft also started approaching customers telling them they’d cancel licenses if Netscape was installed on their hardware which began the events of the breaking antitrust laws.

In addition to having a monopoly on operating systems and software, Microsoft also had a huge surplus of cash which allowed them to weather any financial storm and give them the ability to release their browser for free while bundled within Windows OS which for Netscape was the beginning of the end. Up until this point Netscape charged money for the use of their software which allowed the company to continue development, but when Microsoft released the free alternative, they had no way to maintain the stream of revenue that they had once sustained. By September 1997, the browser war was over as Internet Explorer made up 80% of all browser users and Netscape had crumbled and was eventually bought out by AOL.

In 1998, Microsoft was found guilty of breaking antitrust laws and was ordered to break the company up, dropping its stock market value by over 30 billion dollars overnight. Later on, a Fed appeals court had made a decision to rescind the order as there were thoughts it was too harsh.
